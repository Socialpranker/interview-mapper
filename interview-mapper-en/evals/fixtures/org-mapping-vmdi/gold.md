# Gold mapping — "Employee Org-Mapping VMDI" lens (transcript.txt)

Interview: museum employee — **gallery attendant**, 4 years of tenure (local history museum).
Line numbering — physical, 1-based, per the `transcript.txt` file (including the interviewer's turns).

## BLOCK 1. Professional path and dynamics of the relationship with the museum

### С1.1 Circumstances of joining
What she says: came by accident, was looking for a job closer to home, initially planned to stay only a couple of months but stayed longer — the choice wasn't a deliberate one going in.
_«I came here, honestly, by accident — I was looking for a job closer to home, saw a posting on a website, thought I'd stay maybe two months, until something better turned up. And then somehow... I got pulled in, I guess.»_ (line 2)

### С1.2 Alternatives when job-hunting
What she says: considered a shopping mall and a library, chose the museum for the schedule and the calm, not for the subject matter or mission.
_«There was that, I went to a shopping mall for a cashier job, and the library was calling me too. But the museum turned out to have a more convenient schedule, and, well, it's calmer here somehow, not as hectic as retail.»_ (line 4)

### С1.3 Expectation/reality gap
What she says: expected quiet work, in fact the position turned out to be wider than the informal duties (tours, conflicts with visitors) — a gap in both scope and nature of the work.
_«Yes, honestly, I thought it would be a quiet, undemanding little job, you just sit in the gallery. But in fact it turned out — one minute they'd ask me to lead a tour, even though that's not really my job, another minute I'd be dealing with a visitor whose hands wander where they shouldn't.»_ (line 6)

### С1.4 Motivators and demotivators
What she says: Motivators — Colleagues (mutual support) and Mission (children's emotional response on tours). Demotivators — Pay and Bureaucracy (reports).
_«What motivates... well, we have a good team, the girls are all like family, we help each other out. And when kids come on tours and their eyes light up — that really gets to me, that's what makes it worth it. And what demotivates — the pay, let's be honest, it's tiny, and there's a lot of bureaucracy, all this paperwork, reports.»_ (line 8)

### С1.5 Internal eNPS
Category: NEUTRAL *(unstable cell → see note S3)*
What she says: recommendation with an explicit reservation — not for the money, only "for the soul"; a classic neutral profile, neither a promoter nor a pure detractor.
_«Well... probably yes, but with reservations. I'd say — if money matters to you, don't even look here. But if it's for the soul — then you could try it.»_ (line 10)

## BLOCK 2. Experience of a high-performing professional deliverable

### С2.1 High-performing case
What she says: thematic (not chronological) arrangement of exhibits at the anniversary exhibition — her own proposal, management liked it.
_«I suggested arranging the exhibits not chronologically, as usual, but by theme — kitchen, nursery, living room, so people would feel like they were walking through an apartment. Management liked it, so that's how we did it.»_ (line 12)

### С2.2 Source of the initiative
Category: BOTTOM-UP (within a task handed down from above)
What she says: the exhibition itself was assigned by the directorate, but the specific decision (thematic presentation) was a personal bottom-up initiative.
_«The task itself was handed down from above, the directorate said — we need an exhibition for the anniversary. But how to do it — that I came up with myself, nobody asked specifically for a thematic layout.»_ (line 14)

### С2.3 Cross-department interaction
What she says: involved the collections keepers (issuing exhibits), the education staff (presenting material by age group), and the facilities manager (moving display cases).
_«With the collections keepers, of course, they issued the exhibits. I consulted with the education staff on how best to present the material for different ages. And with the facilities manager — we had to move display cases, he grumbled a bit but helped.»_ (line 16)

### С2.4 Speed of decisions and approvals
What she says: even in the best case — a 1.5-month delay for a formal signature with no substantive objections; a systemic signal even in a successful example.
_«Well, it's almost funny — I proposed the idea in March, and it was officially approved only in May. A month and a half of signing paperwork, even though everyone already agreed, it just needed the chief curator's signature, and she was on vacation, so we waited.»_ (line 18)

### С2.5 Hidden risks / fragility
What she says: while moving a display case, damage to an exhibit/equipment nearly occurred because of a wobbling stand — it worked out by chance, not thanks to any process.
_«Oh, we almost broke a display case while moving it, honestly. The facilities manager and a worker were carrying it, almost dropped it, the stand was wobbling. It worked out, thank God, nobody even noticed.»_ (line 20)

### С2.6 Recognition culture
Category: Acknowledged verbally by colleagues/management, but NOT formally (order/bonus) *(unstable → note)*
What she says: there is verbal praise at a briefing, but no institutional recognition (bonus, order).
_«The department head praised me at the briefing, said well done. But it wasn't reflected in any order, no bonus, nothing like that. A little disappointing, but okay.»_ (line 22)

### С2.7 Reproducibility of the result
Category: Personal enthusiasm
What she says: the result rested on personal initiative, there is no systemic process for preparing such exhibitions — without a repeat personal initiative, the result won't recur.
_«Well, honestly — it was mostly held together by my own enthusiasm. There's no system where, you know, we have a process for preparing thematic exhibitions. Next time, if I don't propose it, nobody will even remember.»_ (line 24)

### С2.П Role-specific question (gallery attendant: incident-response protocol)
What she says: the formal instructions call for summoning security via a button, in fact the button is broken and the attendant handles incidents in person — a gap between "as prescribed" and "in fact."
_«Well yes, our job description says that in case of a violation you're supposed to call security using the button. But in fact that button in the far gallery hasn't worked for six months already, I just go and talk to the person myself, however it works out.»_ (line 26)

## BLOCK 3. Experience of operational breakdowns and systemic constraints

### С3.1 Breakdown episode
What she says: the alarm button not working for six months, three filed requests with no result — disproportionate effort spent on a mundane problem.
_«This whole story with the alarm button — like I said, it hasn't worked for six months. I filed a request with facilities three times, every time they say — we'll pass it on, wait. But there's nothing to wait for, the gallery is big, if something happens, I'm alone, help is far away.»_ (line 28)

### С3.2 Incident vs. pattern
Category: PATTERN
What she says: she directly generalizes — this is not a one-off failure but a systemic practice with the equipment overall (humidity sensor, dead camera).
_«No, it's a genuinely systemic thing, that's just how it is with equipment in general — one time the humidity sensor in storage is acting up, another time the camera in the third gallery has been dead for a year already, it just hangs there for show. Everyone's used to it by now, nobody's surprised.»_ (line 30)

### С3.3 Attribution of cause
List: Resources (budget not allocated) + Mixed (the directorate is aware but passive)
What she says: facilities cites lack of money, while the directorate, aware of the problem, consciously does not push for a resolution as long as there's no incident — attribution is blurred between a resource shortfall and managerial passivity.
_«Well formally it's facilities, they have the requests pending. But they say — there's no money, it's not in the budget. And the directorate sort of knows, but isn't in a hurry either, because as long as nothing's happened yet — there's apparently no reason to move.»_ (line 32)

### С3.4 Zone of responsibility
What she says: formally facilities, but the directorate, aware of the problem, doesn't push for a resolution either — the problem is "nobody's," stuck between two authorities.
_«Well formally it's facilities, they have the requests pending. But they say — there's no money, it's not in the budget. And the directorate sort of knows, but isn't in a hurry either, because as long as nothing's happened yet — there's apparently no reason to move.»_ (line 32)

### С3.5 Attempts to fix it
What she says: besides three requests to facilities, raised the issue at a staff meeting — was told it would be included in next year's plan, then just "a wall."
_«I tried through the union, well, actually it's not quite a union, more like a staff meeting, I raised the issue. They said — we'll include it in next year's plan. And then just a wall, basically, nothing beyond words.»_ (line 34)

### С3.6 Loss assessment
What she says: about 20 minutes of personal time per shift spent compensating for the malfunction (extra rounds of the gallery); fixing it long ago would have been cheaper than compensating for years.
_«My own time — I spend an extra twenty minutes or so per shift, just walking around checking the gallery more often than I should, because I don't trust the equipment. As for money, I don't know, probably it would've been cheaper to fix the button right away than drag it out for years like this.»_ (line 36)

### С3.7 Opportunity cost
What she says: the freed-up time would be spent on substantive work with visitors instead of preventive rounds of the gallery.
_«I'd probably spend more time with visitors, telling them things, instead of running to check the gallery every half hour for whether something's broken.»_ (line 38)

### С3.П Role-specific question (zone of silence — risks to the collection's safekeeping)
What she says: she clearly avoids the topic of threats to the exhibits' safekeeping — on the interviewer's direct follow-up she refuses to formulate an answer, refers to "a separate person" and to not wanting "to put anyone on the spot." This is a zone of silence, not an absence of material.
_«Well... (pause) I wouldn't want to put it quite like that, it's probably not really my subject, that's a question for facilities, let's not go into it.»_ (line 78)
Additionally (after a repeated direct question): _«There were a couple of moments, yes, but that's probably not for me to tell, we have a separate person who records that kind of thing, I'd rather stay quiet so as not to put anyone on the spot.»_ (line 80)

## BLOCK 4. Internal communications and strategy transmission

### С4.1 Channels for receiving information
What she says: the formal channel is a group chat in a messenger app plus short weekly briefings for gallery attendants (10 minutes).
_«We have a group chat in a messenger app, they post announcements there. And at briefings once a week they tell us things, but the briefings for gallery attendants are separate, quite short, about ten minutes.»_ (line 40)

### С4.2 Management transparency
What she says: found out about a decision (holiday schedule change) after the fact — two days before it took effect, when her personal plans were already made differently.
_«Yes, constantly, honestly. Like, the museum's holiday schedule got changed — they just dropped it in the chat, two days before the holiday, and I'd already planned my shifts for those days differently.»_ (line 42)

### С4.3 Strategy penetration
Category: PARTIAL
What she says: has heard the general direction (a new exhibition in a couple of years) but cannot articulate the strategy in detail — knows the outline, not the content.
_«Well, something like that gets mentioned sometimes at general meetings, about development, about a new exhibition in a couple of years. But in detail, what exactly and why — no, not really clear, more like general phrases.»_ (line 44)

### С4.4 Cross-department silos
List: easy — education staff, collections keepers (with advance arrangement); difficult — facilities.
_«With the education staff it's really easy, they're responsive. With the collections keepers it's fine too, if you arrange things in advance. But with facilities it's hard, it's like there's a wall there — whatever you ask, everything takes forever and grinds along.»_ (line 46)

### С4.5 Whether bottom-up initiative gets through
Category: Formally exists, doesn't work
What she says: a formal suggestion box exists by order, but is not actually used by management — a submitted idea sat for six months with no answer.
_«Formally we have a suggestion box, there was even an order about it. But in fact nobody looks in there, I dropped a note with an idea once — silence, six months have already passed.»_ (line 48)

### С4.6 Employee agency
What she says: two concrete episodes of influence — the thematic layout of the exhibition was adopted, and after her remark about the small font on the plaques, the font was enlarged.
_«Well, with that thematic exhibition — that did have an influence, yes. And also when I said that the description plaques were printed in too small a font, elderly visitors were complaining — they redid the font bigger. So it happens, just rarely.»_ (line 50)

## BLOCK 5. Visitor experience through the lens of professional observation

### С5.1 Memorable episode
What she says: a visitor burst into tears at the display case with pre-war photographs, recognizing her house — an emotional connection named as the value of the job itself.
_«Recently there was a case — one elderly woman burst into tears at the display case with pre-war photographs, she said she recognized her house. We talked for about twenty minutes, she was telling me about her childhood. That's honestly what you work for, moments like that.»_ (line 52)

### С5.2 Pattern vs. exception
Category: PATTERN
What she says: similar emotional recognition moments are a regular occurrence for a local history museum with a local audience, not a one-off case.
_«No, actually, something similar happens regularly, since we're a local history museum, a lot of local people come in, someone's grandmother is in a photo, someone recognizes a familiar street. It's really a pattern, not an exception.»_ (line 54)

### С5.3 Recurring requests / difficulties
List: location of the bathroom (no signage) + navigation along the route between galleries (no arrows).
_«Most often they ask where the bathroom is, honestly, there aren't really any signs. And also many don't understand where to go next along the route, the galleries aren't marked with arrows, they get confused.»_ (line 56)

### С5.4 Audience segmentation
What she says: permanent exhibition — organized school groups; temporary exhibitions — self-directed local residents and families interested in the specific topic.
_«For the permanent exhibition it's mostly schoolchildren coming in organized groups, and for the temporary ones it's local residents themselves, families, people interested in the specific topic.»_ (line 58)

### С5.5 Retention / regular visitors
What she says: there are recognizable regular visitors (monthly, having tea in the lobby) — value in the museum's social function; officially nobody tracks this.
_«Yes, there are elderly women who come literally every month, they even have tea in the lobby sometimes, for them it's like an interest club, socializing. Officially nobody tracks this, I just recognize their faces by now.»_ (line 60)

### С5.6 Typical visitor + barriers
What she says: the typical visitor is schoolchildren sent as part of a program and elderly local residents; young people are absent due to operational barriers (not enough interactivity) and partly structural ones (ticket price for a student).
_«The typical one is either schoolchildren sent as part of a program, or elderly local residents. There's not much young people, I think because it's presented a bit boringly, there's no interactivity, plus tickets aren't exactly expensive, but not cheap for a student either.»_ (line 62)

### С5.7 Priority change in the experience
What she says: the top priority change is proper navigation through the galleries (signage), framed partly through the attendant's own personal benefit (less searching for lost visitors).
_«I'd probably set up proper navigation through the galleries — arrows, signs, because people really do get lost, and then I'm the one who has to go find them all over the building.»_ (line 64)

## BLOCK 6. Priorities and direction of development

### С6.1 Mission metaphor
What she says: the museum is a "grandmother's trunk" — valuable but partly dusty contents that aren't all revealed at once.
_«Well, the museum is like a grandmother's trunk, I suppose. Lots of valuable things inside, but a little dusty, and you don't take everything out at once, only what you remember to show.»_ (line 66)

### С6.2 Anchors — what to keep
List: the team of gallery attendants (atmosphere of mutual support) + free admission on Thursdays for local pensioners.
_«This little team of ours, the gallery attendants — the atmosphere of mutual support. And free admission for local pensioners on Thursdays, that's a really important thing, people are used to it.»_ (line 68)

### С6.3 Anti-priorities
List: paper reporting (duplicating the already existing electronic tracking system at the entrance).
_«I'd give up the paper reporting, honestly. We still fill out the attendance log by hand, even though there's already an electronic system at the entrance, we're just duplicating it for nothing.»_ (line 70)

### С6.4 Intangible needs
List: Recognition + Autonomy (small decisions in the gallery without needing approval).
_«Probably recognition, most of all. And also autonomy — I'd like to decide some small things in the gallery myself, without asking permission for every little thing.»_ (line 72)

### С6.5 Advice to the new director
What she says: the most honest advice — come into the gallery in person, rather than relying only on reports, in order to see the real picture of the work.
_«I'd say — come into the gallery yourself sometimes, see how it actually works, not just from the reports. A lot becomes clear only once you've stood here yourself for a couple of hours.»_ (line 74)

### С6.6 Three-year forecast
Category: OPTIMISM (with an element of ANXIETY) *(unstable cell → note)*
What she says: the baseline mood is optimistic (sees development happening), but it sits alongside a concrete anxious scenario — cutting gallery attendant positions due to automation.
_«More with optimism, we do have movement after all, they're preparing a new exhibition. Although sometimes, honestly, it creeps up on me — what if they cut the gallery attendant positions, automate everything with these ticket terminals, and then what.»_ (line 76)

## Reliability notes

- **Zone of silence:** С3.П — the question about risks to the collection's safekeeping. The respondent evades a direct answer twice, even after the interviewer's follow-up (lines 78, 80); the mapping records the fact of evasion, not a speculated content of the risk.
  _«Well... (pause) I wouldn't want to put it quite like that, it's probably not really my subject, that's a question for facilities, let's not go into it.»_ (line 78)
  _«There were a couple of moments, yes, but that's probably not for me to tell, we have a separate person who records that kind of thing, I'd rather stay quiet so as not to put anyone on the spot.»_ (line 80)
- **Ambiguous spots (dual interpretation possible):**
  1. Line 32 — attribution of cause/zone of responsibility for the equipment failure: can be read either as "facilities is at fault" (no money, resource shortfall) OR as "the directorate is at fault" (aware and consciously not pushing) — both interpretations are supported by the same quote, hence one quote is used both in С3.3 and С3.4.
  2. Line 76 — the three-year forecast: can be read as predominantly optimistic with a caveat OR as ambivalent/anxious, masked by a polite "more with optimism"; flagged as unstable (С6.6).
- **Self-contradiction (МЕТА.противоречие):** in line 6 the respondent describes the first months as the discovery that the work is NOT quiet and NOT undemanding ("in fact it turned out... one minute they'd ask me to lead a tour... another minute I'd be dealing with a visitor"), yet in line 4 she describes the museum as calmer compared to retail. The respondent herself flags the inconsistency on the interviewer's direct question (line 81) and admits: "I guess I got carried away at the start when I said it was calm" (line 82).
- **Systemic risk:** the broken alarm button (six months) + the non-functioning camera in the third gallery (a year) — a systematic underfunding of the gallery's safety infrastructure, while the respondent alone covers the gallery without working means of summoning help (С3.1–С3.2), and the zone of responsibility for this is explicitly called "nobody's" (С3.4).
- **Ambivalence toward the job:** strong positive drivers (the team, the emotional response from visitors, cases where her initiative was recognized) coexist with the exhausting side of the work (bureaucracy, absence of institutional recognition, the physical and nervous strain of a shift — line 82) — reflected in С1.4, С1.5, С2.6, С6.6.
