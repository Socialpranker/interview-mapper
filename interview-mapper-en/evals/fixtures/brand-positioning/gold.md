# Gold mapping: "Positioning / brand / naming" lens

**Case:** the "Perky" coffee chain (grab-and-go, locations near offices). Competitors mentioned in the interview — "Coffee Cult" (more grown-up, "tasting" image) and "BlackCat" (minimalist, black cups). The respondent is a regular customer of "Perky" (goes almost every weekday) and an occasional customer of "Coffee Cult"; he only knows about "BlackCat" from his friend's account.

## Layer 1 — operational

**P1. Associations with the current brand**
The first spontaneous reaction (before any leading questions) is a sharp alarm clock and school gym class — an image of being pushed, not of relaxing: "the first thing that pops into my head is, like, some alarm clock, that sharp ringing sound" (line 4); "I also remember gym class in school, I can literally picture that gym" (line 5). The stated tone here is neutral: "a fine brand, nothing weird, the name's just a bit funny" (line 9) — but this is at odds with the emotional marker "being hurried along" a bit further in the text (see A4/hidden tension).

**P2. What the name conveys vs. hides**
It conveys the idea of energy/perking up: "It conveys, basically, that it's about energy, about perking up and running off back to work" (line 27). It hides the actual product quality and the baristas' work: "doesn't convey at all is that the coffee there is actually good, I mean the baristas are solid" (line 28). The word itself is hard to recall and gets replaced by a description of the place in conversation: "the name just doesn't come to mind on its own" (line 32).

**P3. Name options**
"Jolt" and "Pulse" are proposed: "something like \"Jolt\" or \"Pulse,\" short, punchy" (line 34). "Jolt" is accepted because it keeps the idea of energy without the "schoolteacher-ish" undertone of an alarm clock: "I like \"Jolt\" because it keeps that idea of energy" (line 35). An option with a foreign word ("Energy Cup") is explicitly rejected as sounding cheap: "not go with something with a foreign word like \"Energy Cup\"" (line 39).

**P4. References**
Outside the category — the burger chain "Crafthouse," valued for its minimalism and black-and-white signage: "I like how it's done at this one burger chain, called \"Crafthouse,\" they've got minimalism in the signage, black-and-white, stylish" (line 41). The global category reference is Starbucks as "the first thing that comes to mind": "Starbucks obviously, just because that's the first thing everyone thinks of for a coffee brand at all" (line 44).

**P5. Identity and visuals**
The logo is a yellow lightning bolt on a mug, perceived as loud/acid-colored: "a yellow lightning bolt on a mug, pretty loud, I'd say straight-up acid yellow" (line 46). The physical signage is in poor condition: "The sign at the actual location has already faded a bit, the letters are peeling on the left" (line 47). The website is outdated, and social media is clearly run without much resourcing: "Honestly their website's so-so, I went there once to check the menu, and it was just some old version sitting there" (line 49).

**P6. Recognition and audience**
There's a name-recognition problem specifically, even though visual recognition is intact: "if I tell someone \"Perky,\" half of them ask what that even is" (line 52). The stated target audience is office workers grabbing coffee to go: "it's for office people who are rushing in the morning, strictly grab-and-go, quick and off to work" (line 55). There's a gap with the actual audience — students come in the evening to "sit around," which doesn't match the place's style: "in reality students also go there in the evening to sit around, and that's strange, because the sign and the whole style are absolutely not about sitting around" (line 56).

**P7. Renaming process**
The recommendation is to ask regular customers first, not change things abruptly: "asking regular customers, not just go and swap out the sign overnight" (line 60). The risk of an abrupt change is that people won't recognize it's the same place and will walk past out of habit: "some people just won't realize it's the same place, and they'll walk right past out of habit" (line 61).

## Layer 2 — analytical

**A1. The brand's core essence (unstable)**
The respondent himself cannot reduce the brand to a single essence and clearly contradicts himself over the course of the interview: at one point he calls "Perky" just quick coffee with no frills, at another a place that genuinely experiments with seasonal drinks, and ultimately admits it's unresolved: "I don't even know myself, is it a checkbox assembly line or a place that genuinely loves coffee, depends on the day, I guess" (line 70). Flagging this as UNSTABLE: the interpretation isn't singular — it can be read either as "the brand hasn't found itself yet" or as "the brand organically combines speed and quality," and the line depends on the weight an analyst gives to each of the two statements (lines 67–68 vs. 70).

**A2. Gap between current and desired positioning**
The stated positioning (grab-and-go for rushed office workers) doesn't match the actual usage (evening student visitors who linger) — the respondent himself frames this as the brand's own indecision about its audience: "it's like they haven't figured out for themselves who this place is actually for" (line 58).

**A3. Rebranding risk — HIGH**
Recognition currently rests not on the name but on the visual code (yellow color, lightning bolt) built up over three years; changing the name while keeping the visuals risks confusion, and changing the visuals risks losing recognition altogether: "if you suddenly change the name but keep the colors and the lightning bolt, everyone will just get confused, that's part of the recognition too" (line 64).

**A4. Fault line in opinions**
The sources behind impressions of different brands are clearly distinguishable and carry different reliability: for "Perky" and "Coffee Cult" — personal experience ("I go almost every weekday," line 12; "I get ... sometimes too," line 16), for "BlackCat" — a friend's secondhand account, not personally verified: "my friend told me about that one, that it's all really stylish there, black cups, that kind of minimalism" (line 17). This is a fault line between "I know it myself" and "I heard/saw it secondhand" — these sources shouldn't be blended when synthesizing conclusions about competitors.

**A5. Key insight**
The brand is perceived as grab-and-go energy for rushed office customers, but the name is hard to recall and doesn't convey the product's actual quality, while recognition rests on the visual code rather than the name — so renaming is possible, but it should preserve the current visual code and proceed gradually, grounded in regular customers' opinions rather than an abrupt sign swap: anchor quote "the name just doesn't come to mind on its own" (line 32).
