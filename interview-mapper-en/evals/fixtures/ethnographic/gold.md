# Gold Mapping — Ethnographic/Contextual Lens (in-situ)

Observation: senior picker at an auto-parts online store warehouse, picking order
#48213 (three line items: bearings, brake pads, oil filter) in the WarehousePro WMS system.
One-off session, the respondent herself flags the atypicality of the day (warehouse after
an inventory count, line 4) — an open question for high-stakes decisions: whether further
observation is needed.

  Quote: "here's my terminal, I scan the bin barcode, then the item barcode" (line 6);

- **N2 Observed sequence of actions** — opened the picking task for order 48213 (line 8),

## Layer 2 — Analytical

- **A1 Adaptation type (N3)** — *(unstable cell)*
  - Warehouse map notebook (N3.1) — PROCESS-FAILURE WORKAROUND: compensates for the mismatch
    between the system's bin addresses and the item's actual location. Basis: "in the system
    the bin addresses periodically don't match what's really on the shelf" (line 14).
  - Terminal-freeze sticky note (N3.2) — PROCESS/SYSTEM-FAILURE WORKAROUND: compensates for a
    known terminal bug. Basis: "if you scan the same box twice in a row, the terminal
    freezes" (line 21).
  - Handwritten box sticker (N3.3) — ambiguous: can be read either as a workaround for a label
    printing failure (the printer prints small and crooked), or as an excessive precaution/
    entrenched shift habit ("all of us senior pickers do this... it just sort of caught on" —
    line 67). Cannot be classified definitively without data on the packers' error rate —
    flagged as an open question.

- **A2 Significance of the says↔does gap (N5)** — *(assumption, not fact)*
  For the route case (lines 10—11), it looks more like the respondent genuinely doesn't
  notice the gap: the phrase "no improvising" is delivered confidently and without
  hesitation right before the observer recorded the opposite, which points more toward an
  automated habit that the respondent herself doesn't flag as a deviation. For the
  fast-pick-zone case (lines 55—56, 61), the gap is, by contrast, conscious and socially
  calibrated: the respondent directly admits "with the supervisor around I'd probably scan
  the bin just to keep things proper, but without him not always" (line 61) — this is closer
  to a deliberate adjustment for the observer/authority figure than to an unconscious
  discrepancy. The distinction between the two cases isn't always sharp given the context —
  keeping this as a flagged assumption, not a fact.

- **A3 Process fragility** — the picking process visibly runs on homegrown adaptations: the
  respondent herself assesses the dependency directly — "if you took my notebook away and
  stripped off the stickers, I'd probably work way slower for the first week, until I
  relearned where everything is" (line 69); observation confirms that over the session she
  "consulted the notebook seven times and the monitor stickers twice, never once opened the
  official picking instructions" (line 70). Replacing the respondent or removing her personal
  artifacts is a risk of a sharp drop in picking speed.

- **A4 Design implication** — at least three mismatches between the official process and
  actual use: 1) bin address records in the WMS diverge from the item's actual location
  (lines 14, 32—33) — address updates aren't keeping pace with restocking moves; 2) the
  official path for fixing discrepancies (a report through the inventory clerk) is too slow
  relative to the pace of work — "Through the report that's about a two-hour wait, through
  the chat it's usually five minutes" (line 38) — the shift's informal chat effectively
  replaces the accounting procedure; 3) the no-exceptions "bin → item" scanning procedure
  doesn't account for the fast-pick zone, where scanning the bin is physically redundant
  (lines 54—56).

- **A5 Key insight** — In actual practice, the picker runs picking off her own hand-drawn
  warehouse map and the shift's informal chat instead of the formal route and the WMS
  discrepancy report, because the system's bin addresses regularly diverge from the item's
  actual location, and the official procedure for correcting discrepancies (a report through
  the clerk) is far slower than what's needed to hit the picking quota; the systemic
  consequence is that WMS records chronically lag reality ("we find about ten of these
  discrepancies per shift", line 44), and the resilience of the entire picking process
  effectively rests on one employee's personal artifacts and informal channels rather than
  on the system itself.

## Limitation (Hawthorne / single observation)

The respondent herself directly names the observer effect: "I'm working more carefully than
usual today, because you're standing right here, normally I'd probably cut a few corners
that I felt too embarrassed to show just now" (line 74), and gives a concrete example of
normally hidden behavior — picking two orders in parallel instead of one (line 77), which
was not observed live at the time of the conversation (observation, line 78: "the respondent
is standing with one box in hand, no second box visible nearby" — meaning the example was
given retrospectively, not shown in action). This confirms that a single observation session
systematically underestimates the real scale of deviations from procedure — an open question
for any high-stakes decision based on this observation.
